The Complete Mission Trip Planning Checklist

A timeline-based checklist covering every phase of mission trip planning — from the first leadership meeting to the final debrief. Built from years of real trip experience.

Planning a mission trip involves hundreds of details, and the difference between a smooth trip and a chaotic one usually comes down to how far in advance you start and whether anything falls through the cracks.

This mission trip planning checklist breaks the entire process into time-based phases. Work through them in order and you will arrive at your departure date with a fully funded, fully prepared, and spiritually grounded team. Print it, share it with your co-leaders, or use it as a template for your own planning workflow.

Phase 1

6+ Months Before the Trip

This is the foundation phase. Get your leadership, destination, and finances locked in before you open it up to the broader team.

  • Form your trip leadership team. Identify a trip leader and 1-2 co-leaders. Assign clear responsibilities early — who handles logistics, finances, and spiritual preparation. You need at least two people who can make decisions independently.
  • Choose your destination and ministry partner. If you are working with an in-country partner or organization, begin that relationship now. Confirm what kind of team they need, housing capacity, and any project-specific skills required.
  • Set your trip dates. Work backward from your target departure to build your planning timeline. Avoid dates that conflict with school schedules if youth are involved. Lock dates early so flights stay affordable.
  • Establish the trip budget. Estimate per-person costs including flights, housing, meals, ground transport, project materials, travel insurance, and a contingency buffer (10-15%). This number drives everything else. GO's budget tracking lets you set this up from day one and track every dollar in and out through the entire trip.
  • Define your fundraising strategy. Decide whether participants self-fund, fundraise, or some combination. Set individual fundraising goals and determine how you will handle team-wide donations (split evenly or allocated by need).
  • Get church leadership approval. Present the trip proposal to your pastor, missions committee, or church board. Include the destination, dates, budget, team size, and ministry objectives. Get this in writing.
  • Research travel requirements. Check passport requirements (most countries require 6 months validity beyond your return date), visa needs, and CDC vaccination recommendations for your destination. Flag anything that requires lead time.
Phase 2

4-5 Months Before the Trip

With the framework in place, start building your team and booking the big-ticket items.

  • Open team applications. Create a clear application with questions about availability, health considerations, passport status, and why they want to go. Set a firm application deadline. GO's trip application form handles this online — applicants fill it out and you review and approve from your dashboard.
  • Book flights. International flights are typically cheapest 4-5 months out. Book as a group if possible for easier coordination. Confirm that every team member's passport name matches their legal name exactly.
  • Secure housing and in-country logistics. Confirm accommodations with your ministry partner or book lodging. Clarify what is included (meals, transport, translators) and what you need to arrange separately.
  • Launch fundraising. Open your donation page and equip each team member with a personal fundraising link and a support letter template. Coach them on how to ask — most people are bad at it until someone shows them. GO generates individual donation pages for each team member and tracks progress with milestone deadlines and stoplight indicators.
  • Collect passports (or confirm passport status). Anyone who needs a passport or renewal should apply immediately. Expedited processing still takes 4-6 weeks, and standard processing can take 8-12 weeks. Do not assume people have this handled.
  • Schedule your first team meeting. Cover the trip vision, expectations, fundraising plan, and upcoming deadlines. This is also where you set the tone — a mission trip is not a vacation, and that message needs to land early.
  • Begin background checks. If your church requires background checks for anyone working with minors or vulnerable populations (and it should), start this process now. Some checks take 2-3 weeks.
Phase 3

2-3 Months Before the Trip

The team is forming. Now it is time to lock in details, collect paperwork, and start preparing hearts and minds.

  • Finalize the team roster. Close applications, confirm every accepted member, and communicate final decisions. If you have a waitlist, let those people know their status clearly.
  • Book ground transportation. Arrange airport transfers, daily transport to ministry sites, and any internal travel. Get quotes from your ministry partner or research local rental companies. Always have a backup plan.
  • Collect medical and emergency information. Gather health conditions, allergies, medications, emergency contacts, and insurance information for every team member. A leader should carry printed copies of this during the trip.
  • Collect signed liability waivers and release forms. Every participant (and the parent/guardian of every minor) needs to sign a liability waiver before departure. Do not let this slide to the last minute. GO's e-signature feature handles waivers digitally, including guardian co-signatures for minors via email link. Automatic reminders keep people on track.
  • Assign team roles and responsibilities. Designate a trip photographer, a daily devotional leader, a first aid point person, a budget tracker, and any project-specific roles. People engage more when they own something.
  • Start pre-trip devotionals and team building. Meet regularly (bi-weekly works well) for Bible study focused on the mission. Study the culture and history of your destination. Pray together for the people you will serve. GO integrates with MKDSCPLS discipleship tools — you can assign mission-focused reading plans and track team spiritual preparation alongside logistics.
  • Check vaccination and medication timelines. Some vaccinations require multiple doses over several weeks. Malaria prophylaxis often needs to start 1-2 weeks before travel. Consult a travel medicine clinic now.
  • Review fundraising progress. Identify anyone who is significantly behind on their fundraising goal. Connect them with resources, help them write follow-up letters, or explore scholarship options from the church missions budget.
Phase 4

1 Month Before the Trip

The countdown is on. Everything should be converging — finances, documents, logistics, and team readiness.

  • Final fundraising push. Send a "last call" update to supporters. Share the team's progress and highlight specific needs. Churches often see a surge in giving in the final weeks when the urgency is real.
  • Confirm all documents are signed and collected. Waivers, medical forms, passport copies, flight confirmations — do a full audit. Chase down anything missing now, not the week of departure. GO's document dashboard shows exactly who has signed and who has not, with overdue indicators and one-click reminders.
  • Distribute packing lists. Create a detailed packing list tailored to your destination and project. Include work clothes, modest clothing guidelines, required gear, personal medications, and items to leave at home. Be specific.
  • Hold a team orientation meeting. Walk through the full itinerary, cultural expectations, safety protocols, communication plan, and daily schedule. This is the meeting where you answer every "what if" question.
  • Finalize the daily itinerary. Build out a day-by-day schedule with ministry activities, meal plans, devotional times, and free time. Share it with your in-country partner for feedback and confirmation. GO's itinerary builder lets you create day-by-day schedules that the whole team can access on any device.
  • Set up a communication plan. Decide how the team will communicate during the trip (WhatsApp group, satellite communicator, etc.) and how you will send updates to families and the church back home. Test everything before you leave.
Phase 5

1-2 Weeks Before Departure

Final details. Everything on this list should take minutes, not hours — if it does not, something earlier slipped.

  • Create emergency contact cards. Print a card for each team member with the trip leader's local phone number, the hotel/housing address, the U.S. embassy contact, and any in-country emergency numbers. Laminate them if you can.
  • Confirm travel insurance. Verify that every team member has travel medical insurance and emergency evacuation coverage. Many standard health plans do not cover international emergencies. Do not skip this.
  • Handle currency exchange. Research whether to exchange before departure or use ATMs in-country. Notify your bank of international travel dates. Carry some cash in local currency for arrival-day expenses.
  • Hold a final team meeting and commissioning. Pray over the team with the church congregation if possible. This is meaningful for the team and for the church body sending them. Review last-minute logistics and answer final questions.
  • Medication and vaccination check. Confirm everyone who needs malaria prophylaxis has started it on schedule. Verify that anyone with prescription medications has enough supply plus a buffer. Pack a well-stocked first aid kit.
  • Make copies of critical documents. Photocopy every team member's passport, visa, insurance card, and flight itinerary. Keep one set with the trip leader and leave one set with a contact at home.
Phase 6

On the Trip

The planning pays off here. Your job as a leader shifts from logistics to shepherding the team through the experience.

  • Daily team devotionals. Start each day grounded in Scripture and prayer. Keep it short (15-20 minutes) but consistent. Let different team members lead. Process what God is doing in real time.
  • Track expenses as they happen. Record every purchase the day it happens — not three days later from memory. Photograph receipts. Designate one person to own this so nothing slips. GO's expense tracking lets you log costs on the ground from any device, keeping your budget balance current throughout the trip.
  • Document the trip with photos and journals. Assign a team photographer for each day. Encourage everyone to journal daily — even a few sentences. These become the foundation for your post-trip storytelling and donor thank-you updates.
  • Leader check-ins. Touch base with your co-leaders daily (ideally in the evening). Discuss how the team is doing emotionally, physically, and spiritually. Address any conflicts or concerns immediately — they only grow if ignored.
  • Send updates to supporters back home. Post trip updates, photos, and prayer requests for the families and donors who made this possible. Even a short daily update goes a long way. It keeps your support base invested in the mission.
  • Monitor team health and energy. Watch for signs of dehydration, heat exhaustion, culture shock, or emotional overwhelm. Build rest into the schedule. A burned-out team is not an effective team.
Phase 7

After the Trip

The trip is not over when the plane lands. What happens in the weeks after determines whether the experience creates lasting impact or fades into a memory.

  • Hold a team debrief within one week. Gather the full team while the experience is fresh. Let everyone share highs, lows, and what God taught them. Process the re-entry — coming home is often harder than people expect.
  • Send thank-you notes to every donor. Personalized is better, but even a well-written group update is essential. Include photos, a summary of what the team accomplished, and how their donation made it possible. Do this within two weeks.
  • Complete financial reconciliation. Account for every dollar — donations received, expenses paid, and remaining balance. Prepare a summary for your church finance team or missions committee. Transparency builds trust for the next trip. GO's financial reports show total gross, Stripe fees, net received, checks and cash, expenses, and available balance — ready for your finance team.
  • Share the story with your church. Present to the congregation — a Sunday testimony, a video recap, or a missions night. Let team members share in their own words. This inspires the next generation of mission trip participants.
  • Follow up with your ministry partner. Send a thank-you to your in-country hosts. Ask what went well and what could improve. If this is an ongoing partnership, begin discussing next year's dates and needs.
  • Check in on your team at 30 and 90 days. Post-trip depression is real. Some team members will struggle to reconcile what they saw abroad with their daily life at home. A simple check-in call or coffee meeting can make a significant difference.
  • Document lessons learned. Write down what worked, what did not, and what you would change. This becomes the starting playbook for your next trip. Future you will be grateful.
